Choose two

Most hard choices aren't a straight line between good and bad. They're a triangle: three things you want, and room for two.

A trilemma is an honest map of a trade-off. Name the three things you're really after, accept that you can hold at most two, and the decision gets clearer - you stop hoping for all three and start choosing which corner to give up. We tend to do this badly by default: we pick two without noticing, then quietly resent losing the third.

Below are trilemmas from computing, economics, politics and ordinary life - some proven with maths, some just true. Tap two corners of any triangle to see what you keep, and what you have to let go.

(A few famous trilemmas are the opposite shape - three exits and you must take exactly one, like Agrippa's ancient problem of how any belief can be justified. These are all the choose-two kind.)
